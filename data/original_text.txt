A 45-year-old male presented to the emergency department with sudden-onset chest pain radiating to the left arm and jaw, along with shortness of breath and diaphoresis. An electrocardiogram (ECG) was performed, which revealed ST-segment elevation in the anterior leads, consistent with an acute myocardial infarction. The patient was immediately taken to the catheterization laboratory for percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI), where a significant blockage in the left anterior descending artery was identified and successfully treated with stent placement. The patient was transferred to the intensive care unit for post-procedural monitoring and recovery.

Postoperatively, the patient was transferred to the neurointensive care unit for close monitoring.

A 70-year-old female with a history of chronic kidney disease and hypertension presented to the clinic with complaints of generalized weakness, swelling in her legs, and decreased urine output. Laboratory tests revealed worsening renal function with elevated serum creatinine and potassium levels. A renal ultrasound was performed, which showed evidence of bilateral hydronephrosis, suggestive of obstructive uropathy. The patient was admitted to the hospital for further evaluation, and a nephrology consult was obtained. The treatment plan included placement of bilateral ureteral stents to relieve the obstruction and prevent further renal deterioration.
